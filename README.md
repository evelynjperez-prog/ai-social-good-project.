# Wildfire Smoke and Bad Air Quality in San Jose
### 1. Problem and Who it Affects
The problem addressed in this project is wildfire smoke and poor air quality in San Jose. Wildfire smoke contains harmful toxins that can negatively affect people’s health when breathed in. Exposure to smoke can lead to coughing, difficulty breathing, respiratory problems, and other illnesses. Many people may not fully understand how serious the health risks connected to wildfire smoke and poor air quality can be.

This issue is also connected to Climate Action because warmer and drier conditions are contributing to an increase in wildfires. As wildfires become more common, smoke and air pollution continue to impact surrounding communities. Even when indoors, smoke can still enter buildings through windows, doors, and vents, making it difficult to completely avoid exposure. In some cases, people may also remain outdoors without realizing the dangers associated with unhealthy air quality.

This project focuses on raising awareness about the health risks caused by wildfire smoke and poor air quality in San Jose. By bringing attention to these environmental hazards, the project highlights the importance of climate awareness, public safety, and protecting community health from the growing effects of wildfires and air pollution.
### 2. AI Capability Used and Why It Fits
The project utilizes two AI abilities from the labs.

First, Lab 2 uses AI to read written messages about environmental hazards like wildfire smoke and poor air quality in San Jose. A resident can describe what they are experiencing in their own simple words, such as noticing smoke in the air or having trouble breathing. The AI then pulls out key details such as the location, type of problem, level of urgency, and possible health risks like coughing or respiratory issues.

Second, Lab 3 uses AI to understand visual images related to wildfire smoke and air pollution. A resident can upload a photo showing smoky skies or visible air pollution. The AI analyzes the image to recognize signs of wildfire smoke and identifies how severe the air quality issue may be. It can also help communicate the seriousness of the situation in a clear way.

These AI tools fit the problem because many people may not fully understand the risks of wildfire smoke or may not be able to clearly explain what they are experiencing. Instead of long explanations, they can send a short message or an image. The AI then organizes this information so it can better support awareness of air quality issues and help communicate the severity of the situation.
### 3. The Workflow
### Step 1: Input

The input for this project can come from residents in two main ways:

A short written message describing what they are experiencing with wildfire smoke or poor air quality or
A photo showing visible signs of smoke, haze, or other environmental issues

For example, a resident may write that there is heavy wildfire smoke in the area, the air quality is bad, and people are having difficulty breathing or coughing. Another resident may upload a photo showing thick smoke in the air or reduced visibility caused by wildfire smoke.
### Step 2: What the AI Does
The AI reads the written message or analyzes the image to understand the main problem related to wildfire smoke and poor air quality.
For written reports, the AI identifies key details such as:

- Location of the wildfire smoke or poor air quality 
- Type of issue, such as smoke exposure or breathing difficulties caused by air pollution
- Urgency level based on how serious the health risk appears
- Possible health impacts, such as coughing or respiratory issues
- Relevant environmental concern connected to wildfire smoke and toxins in the air

For image reports the AI will identify details such as:
- What is shown, like wildfire smoke or poor air quality
- Whether the situation looks dangerous
- Who may be affected by the smoke
- What action may be needed to respond to the issue
### Step 3: Output
The AI gives an organized report based on wildfire smoke and poor air quality. 
For example, it may say:
Location: San Jose area affected by wildfire smoke

Problem: wildfire smoke and poor air quality

Urgency: high due to health risks like coughing and breathing issues

Department: public health or air quality response team

Suggested action: warn residents and address the air quality hazard
This output can then be used to help communicate the severity of wildfire smoke and support a faster response to air quality issues.
### Step 4: 
After the AI generates the report about wildfire smoke and poor air quality, it is reviewed by a city employee or the appropriate department. Depending on the situation, it may be sent to public health, environmental services, air quality teams, or emergency response units.

Human review is needed to make sure the information is correct. Since AI can sometimes miss details or misinterpret the situation, a person should confirm the report before any action is taken to ensure the wildfire smoke issue and health risks are properly handled.

### 4. Failure Case Tied to a Lab Output
A possible failure case happens when the AI sorts a wildfire smoke and poor air quality report into the wrong department. For example, the issue may describe thick smoke in San Jose causing coughing and difficulty breathing. The AI may still label it as a health concern and send it to public health.

However, if it is assigned to the wrong department, the response may not fully address the situation. For instance, if it only goes to a general city department, the wildfire smoke and air quality problem may not be handled quickly or directly. This leaves the health risk from smoke exposure still affecting people in the area.

This is especially important because wildfire smoke can spread fast and impact breathing health within a short amount of time. If the report is not directed correctly at first, delays can make the situation worse for people already experiencing symptoms like coughing or shortness of breath.

Another failure from Lab 3 is that the AI may not fully understand the seriousness of wildfire smoke just from looking at an image. A photo might clearly show haze or smoke in the sky, but the AI may not always accurately measure how unhealthy or dangerous the air quality is. This shows that AI is helpful for spotting visible smoke, but it can still miss how severe the health impact really is.

### 5. Oversight Decision and One Change
### Oversight Decision

This system should not automatically send reports directly to city departments without human review. A real person should review the AI’s output before any work order is created, especially when the report involves wildfire smoke, poor air quality, health risks, or emergency situations.

The AI could help organize and summarize the information, but it should not make the final decision. A city worker or trained reviewer, such as someone in public health or environmental services, should confirm the urgency and make sure the correct department is assigned before any action is taken.

### Tradeoff

The benefit of having human review is that it helps reduce mistakes in the AI’s decisions. It makes sure wildfire smoke reports are sent to the correct department and that important health risks like breathing issues and poor air quality are not overlooked.

The tradeoff is that adding human review can slow down the process. Reports may take longer to be forwarded compared to a fully automated system. However, in situations involving wildfire smoke and health risks, especially for vulnerable groups, making accurate decisions is more important than speed.

### One Change to Improve the System

One change I would make is to add a clearer priority category for wildfire smoke and poor air quality reports. Instead of treating all environmental issues the same, the system should separate air quality emergencies from regular complaints like trash or minor cleanup.
If a report includes wildfire smoke, coughing, or difficulty breathing, it should automatically be placed in a higher priority level so it gets reviewed faster by the right department.

This change would help ensure that health-related air quality problems are not delayed or overlooked, since wildfire smoke can quickly become dangerous for people in the area.

### Tradeoff

The benefit of adding a clearer priority category is that wildfire smoke and poor air quality reports can be identified and handled faster, helping reduce health risks by getting urgent cases to the right department sooner.

The tradeoff is that it can make the system more complex and harder to manage, and there is still a chance some reports could be placed in the wrong priority level if categories are not clear.
